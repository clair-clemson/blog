Tensor completion asks us to recover a multidimensional array from only a subset of its entries. A color image is a third-order tensor indexed by height, width, and color channel. A grayscale video adds time as a third mode, while a color video is naturally fourth-order. Traffic measurements, medical scans, recommendation data, and spatiotemporal sensor streams have the same structure: the observations are multidimensional, incomplete, and highly correlated.

Let

$$
\mathcal{Y}
\in
\mathbb{R}^{I_1\times I_2\times\cdots\times I_N}
$$

be a partially observed tensor, let $\Omega$ be the set of observed indices, and let $P_\Omega$ keep entries in $\Omega$ while setting all other entries to zero. The goal is to find a tensor $\mathcal{X}$ that agrees with the observed data and has low-rank structure.

The matrix version of this problem has a clean convex model based on the nuclear norm. The tensor version is much harder. Tensor rank is not uniquely defined, a tensor singular value decomposition is substantially more complicated than a matrix SVD, and many basic tensor spectral problems are NP-hard. Simply unfolding a tensor into one large matrix makes the computation easier, but it privileges one mode and can discard useful relationships among the others.

We study a scalable alternative built from four ideas:

- represent the recovered tensor with a canonical polyadic decomposition,
- use every mode unfolding rather than choosing only one,
- replace the expensive tensor nuclear norm with a factorized upper bound,
- optimize one factor at a time with Nesterov acceleration.

The resulting method avoids singular value decomposition inside its main loop, supports tensors of arbitrary order, and has an accelerated $O(1/k^2)$ convergence result under the assumptions used in the paper.

## From Matrix Completion to Tensor Completion

It is useful to begin with the matrix problem. Given a partially observed matrix $\mathbf{Y}$, nuclear-norm-regularized matrix completion solves

$$
\min_{\mathbf{X}}
\quad
\frac{1}{2}
\|P_\Omega(\mathbf{X}-\mathbf{Y})\|_F^2
+
\lambda\|\mathbf{X}\|_*.
$$

The first term fits the observed entries. The nuclear norm

$$
\|\mathbf{X}\|_*
=
\sum_j \sigma_j(\mathbf{X})
$$

is the sum of the singular values and is the standard convex surrogate for matrix rank. Its weakness is computational: evaluating a proximal step for the nuclear norm requires singular value thresholding, which in turn requires an SVD.

The key factorization identity is

$$
\|\mathbf{X}\|_*
=
\min_{\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B}^{\top}=\mathbf{X}}
\frac{1}{2}
\left(
\|\mathbf{A}\|_F^2
+
\|\mathbf{B}\|_F^2
\right).
$$

If $\mathbf{X}=\mathbf{U}\mathbf{\Sigma}\mathbf{V}^{\top}$ is an SVD, the minimum is attained by

$$
\mathbf{A}
=
\mathbf{U}\mathbf{\Sigma}^{1/2},
\qquad
\mathbf{B}
=
\mathbf{V}\mathbf{\Sigma}^{1/2}.
$$

Therefore the matrix problem can be written in the Burer--Monteiro form

$$
\min_{\mathbf{A},\mathbf{B}}
\quad
\frac{1}{2}
\|P_\Omega(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{B}^{\top}-\mathbf{Y})\|_F^2
+
\frac{\lambda}{2}
\left(
\|\mathbf{A}\|_F^2
+
\|\mathbf{B}\|_F^2
\right).
$$

Once the factor width is at least the rank of an optimal solution, the factorized problem and the nuclear-norm problem have the same optimal value. The important computational change is that we now optimize thin factor matrices instead of repeatedly decomposing a large completed matrix.

The tensor method starts from this identity and applies it to all mode unfoldings simultaneously.

## CP Decomposition and Mode Unfoldings

For an $N$th-order tensor

$$
\mathcal{X}
\in
\mathbb{R}^{I_1\times\cdots\times I_N},
$$

a rank-$R$ canonical polyadic, or CP, decomposition is

$$
\mathcal{X}
=
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\boldsymbol{a}_{1,r}
\circ
\boldsymbol{a}_{2,r}
\circ
\cdots
\circ
\boldsymbol{a}_{N,r}.
$$

Here $\circ$ denotes the vector outer product. Collecting the vectors for each mode gives factor matrices

$$
\mathbf{A}_i
=
[\boldsymbol{a}_{i,1},\ldots,\boldsymbol{a}_{i,R}]
\in
\mathbb{R}^{I_i\times R},
$$

and we write the reconstruction compactly as

$$
\mathcal{X}
=
[[\mathbf{A}_1,\mathbf{A}_2,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N]].
$$

The mode-$i$ unfolding $\mathbf{X}_{(i)}$ arranges the mode-$i$ fibers as columns of a matrix. Under the CP model it has the useful factorization

$$
\mathbf{X}_{(i)}
=
\mathbf{A}_i\mathbf{B}_i^{\top},
$$

where

$$
\mathbf{B}_i
=
\mathbf{A}_N
\odot\cdots\odot
\mathbf{A}_{i+1}
\odot
\mathbf{A}_{i-1}
\odot\cdots\odot
\mathbf{A}_1
$$

and $\odot$ denotes the Khatri--Rao product. The dimensions are

$$
\mathbf{B}_i
\in
\mathbb{R}^{(\prod_{j\neq i}I_j)\times R}.
$$

This equation is the bridge from matrix completion to tensor completion. Every unfolding is a matrix product, but all of those products share the same CP factors. Updating one $\mathbf{A}_i$ therefore improves the tensor representation associated with every mode.

## A Unified Nuclear-Norm Surrogate

One common tensor regularizer averages the nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings:

$$
\|\mathcal{X}\|_{\mathrm{unfold},*}
=
\frac{1}{N}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\|\mathbf{X}_{(i)}\|_*.
$$

This gives the completion model

$$
\min_{\mathcal{X}}
\quad
\frac{1}{2}
\|P_\Omega(\mathcal{X}-\mathcal{Y})\|_F^2
+
\frac{\lambda}{N}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\|\mathbf{X}_{(i)}\|_*.
$$

For the CP factors, the matrix identity gives the mode-wise upper bound

$$
\|\mathbf{X}_{(i)}\|_*
\leq
\frac{1}{2}
\left(
\|\mathbf{A}_i\|_F^2
+
\|\mathbf{B}_i\|_F^2
\right).
$$

The second factor does not need to be constructed explicitly. The Gram matrix of a Khatri--Rao product satisfies

$$
\mathbf{B}_i^{\top}\mathbf{B}_i
=
\mathop{*}_{j\neq i}
(\mathbf{A}_j^{\top}\mathbf{A}_j),
$$

where $*$ is the Hadamard product. Consequently,

$$
\|\mathbf{B}_i\|_F^2
=
\operatorname{tr}
\left(
\mathop{*}_{j\neq i}
(\mathbf{A}_j^{\top}\mathbf{A}_j)
\right).
$$

The paper therefore proposes the factorized objective

$$
\begin{aligned}
\min_{\mathbf{A}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N}
\quad
F(\mathbf{A}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N)
=&
\frac{1}{2}
\left\|
P_\Omega
\left(
\mathcal{Y}
-
[[\mathbf{A}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N]]
\right)
\right\|_F^2
\\
&+
\frac{\lambda}{2N}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\left[
\|\mathbf{A}_i\|_F^2
+
\operatorname{tr}
\left(
\mathop{*}_{j\neq i}
(\mathbf{A}_j^{\top}\mathbf{A}_j)
\right)
\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

This objective does two jobs at once.

First, it upper-bounds the average nuclear norm of the unfolding matrices. Each mode wants a different two-factor representation, but the CP factors are shared. For matrices, the factorized identity can be attained exactly. For a tensor of order greater than two, one set of shared factors will generally not attain the nuclear-norm minimum for every unfolding at the same time. The new regularizer is therefore a surrogate, not an equality.

Second, the same expression upper-bounds an atomic definition of the tensor nuclear norm. Suppose

$$
\mathcal{X}
=
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\boldsymbol{v}_{1,r}
\circ\cdots\circ
\boldsymbol{v}_{N,r},
$$

and set

$$
u_{i,r}=\|\boldsymbol{v}_{i,r}\|_2,
\qquad
\mu_r=\prod_{i=1}^{N}u_{i,r}.
$$

After normalizing the nonzero vectors, each rank-one term has coefficient $\mu_r$, so the atomic tensor nuclear norm is at most $\sum_r\mu_r$. For every mode $i$, the scalar inequality

$$
2u_{i,r}
\prod_{j\neq i}u_{j,r}
\leq
u_{i,r}^2
+
\prod_{j\neq i}u_{j,r}^2
$$

implies

$$
\begin{aligned}
\|\mathcal{X}\|_*
&\leq
\sum_{r=1}^{R}\mu_r
\\
&\leq
\frac{1}{2N}
\sum_{r=1}^{R}
\sum_{i=1}^{N}
\left(
u_{i,r}^2
+
\prod_{j\neq i}u_{j,r}^2
\right).
\end{aligned}
$$

Writing $\mathbf{A}\_i=[\boldsymbol{v}\_{i,1},\ldots,\boldsymbol{v}\_{i,R}]$ turns the right-hand side into exactly the factor regularizer above.

This is the central modeling idea: replace a difficult tensor spectral penalty with an SVD-free expression involving only thin factor matrices, their Gram matrices, and Hadamard products.

## Why the New Objective Is Easier

The full objective is not jointly convex because the CP reconstruction multiplies all $N$ factors together. It is, however, convex in one factor when the other factors are fixed.

For a selected mode $i$, define

$$
\mathbf{B}_i
=
\mathop{\odot}_{j\neq i}\mathbf{A}_j
$$

and, for each $\ell\neq i$,

$$
\mathbf{H}_{i,\ell}
=
\mathop{*}_{j\notin\{i,\ell\}}
(\mathbf{A}_j^{\top}\mathbf{A}_j).
$$

Only the diagonal of these Gram products enters the trace. Let

$$
\mathbf{D}_i
=
\operatorname{diag}
\left(
\operatorname{diag}
\left(
\sum_{\ell\neq i}\mathbf{H}_{i,\ell}
\right)
\right).
$$

Holding the other factors fixed, the $i$th block objective is

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_i(\mathbf{A}_i)
=&
\frac{1}{2}
\left\|
P_{\Omega_i}
\left(
\mathbf{Y}_{(i)}
-
\mathbf{A}_i\mathbf{B}_i^{\top}
\right)
\right\|_F^2
\\
&+
\frac{\lambda}{2N}
\operatorname{tr}
\left(
\mathbf{A}_i^{\top}\mathbf{A}_i
(\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{D}_i)
\right),
\end{aligned}
$$

or, equivalently, the regularization part is

$$
\frac{\lambda}{2N}
\left[
\|\mathbf{A}_i\|_F^2
+
\sum_{\ell\neq i}
\operatorname{tr}
\left(
\mathbf{H}_{i,\ell}
*
(\mathbf{A}_i^{\top}\mathbf{A}_i)
\right)
\right].
$$

Its gradient has a compact matrix form:

$$
\nabla F_i(\mathbf{A}_i)
=
P_{\Omega_i}
\left(
\mathbf{A}_i\mathbf{B}_i^{\top}
-
\mathbf{Y}_{(i)}
\right)
\mathbf{B}_i
+
\frac{\lambda}{N}
\mathbf{A}_i(\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{D}_i).
$$

Two properties make accelerated first-order updates possible.

**Lipschitz gradient.** The block gradient is Lipschitz continuous. A convenient upper bound is

$$
L_i
\leq
\|\mathbf{B}_i\|_2^2
+
\frac{\lambda}{N}
\left(
1+\|\mathbf{D}_i\|_2
\right).
$$

Since $\mathbf{D}_i$ is diagonal,

$$
\|\mathbf{D}_i\|_2
=
\max_r [\mathbf{D}_i]_{rr}.
$$

The projection onto observed entries cannot increase the operator norm, which is why $\|\mathbf{B}_i\|_2^2$ controls the data-fit part.

**Strong convexity.** The block Hessian contains the positive regularization term

$$
\frac{\lambda}{N}
(\mathbf{I}+\mathbf{D}_i),
$$

so $F_i$ is strongly convex whenever $\lambda>0$. Its strong-convexity modulus is at least

$$
\mu_i
\geq
\frac{\lambda}{N}
\left(
1+
\min_r[\mathbf{D}_i]_{rr}
\right)
\geq
\frac{\lambda}{N}.
$$

The data-fit term can make this curvature still larger when the observed design in the mode unfolding has full column rank.

This is the computational payoff of the surrogate. Direct nuclear-norm optimization requires spectral operations. The factorized block problem is a smooth, strongly convex quadratic-like problem, so each update needs only a gradient step.

## Accelerated Tensor Completion

The Accelerated Tensor Completion method, abbreviated ATC, performs cyclic factor updates with Nesterov extrapolation. It can use either the analytical Lipschitz bound or a backtracking line search.

**Algorithm: Accelerated Tensor Completion**

**Input:** observed tensor $\mathcal{Y}$, mask $P_\Omega$, regularization parameter $\lambda$, target CP rank $R$

**Output:** restored tensor $\mathcal{X}$

1. Initialize

   $$
   \mathbf{A}_i^0\in\mathbb{R}^{I_i\times R},
   \qquad
   \mathbf{Z}_i^0=\mathbf{A}_i^0,
   \qquad
   i=1,\ldots,N,
   $$

   and set $t_0=1$.

2. For $k=0,1,2,\ldots$ until convergence:

   1. Update the acceleration parameter:

      $$
      t_{k+1}
      =
      \frac{1+\sqrt{1+4t_k^2}}{2}.
      $$

   2. For $i=1,\ldots,N$:

      1. Form the current Khatri--Rao and Gram-product quantities for mode $i$.

      2. Choose the step size

         $$
         s_i^{k+1}
         =
         \frac{1}{L_i^{k+1}}
         $$

         or find it by backtracking.

      3. Take a gradient step from the extrapolated point:

         $$
         \mathbf{A}_i^{k+1}
         =
         \mathbf{Z}_i^k
         -
         s_i^{k+1}
         \nabla F_i(\mathbf{Z}_i^k).
         $$

      4. Extrapolate:

         $$
         \mathbf{Z}_i^{k+1}
         =
         \mathbf{A}_i^{k+1}
         +
         \frac{t_k-1}{t_{k+1}}
         \left(
         \mathbf{A}_i^{k+1}
         -
         \mathbf{A}_i^k
         \right).
         $$

3. Return

   $$
   \mathcal{X}
   =
   [[\mathbf{A}_1^k,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N^k]].
   $$

When the analytical Lipschitz constant is conservative, backtracking can take a larger step. Starting from a trial $s_i$, form

$$
\mathbf{A}_i^+
=
\mathbf{Z}_i
-
s_i\nabla F_i(\mathbf{Z}_i)
$$

and accept the step when the smooth upper-bound condition

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_i(\mathbf{A}_i^+)
\leq
F_i(\mathbf{Z}_i)
&+
\left\langle
\nabla F_i(\mathbf{Z}_i),
\mathbf{A}_i^+-\mathbf{Z}_i
\right\rangle
\\
&+
\frac{1}{2s_i}
\|\mathbf{A}_i^+-\mathbf{Z}_i\|_F^2
\end{aligned}
$$

holds. Otherwise replace $s_i$ by $\eta s_i$ for some $\eta\in(0,1)$ and try again.

## Convergence Argument

The convergence proof uses the standard estimate-sequence mechanism behind Nesterov acceleration.

For a convex differentiable block function $F_i$ with $L_i$-Lipschitz gradient, define

$$
\mathbf{C}
=
\mathbf{B}
-
\frac{1}{L_i}
\nabla F_i(\mathbf{B}).
$$

Then for any comparison point $\mathbf{A}$,

$$
F_i(\mathbf{A})
\geq
F_i(\mathbf{C})
+
L_i
\langle
\mathbf{C}-\mathbf{B},
\mathbf{B}-\mathbf{A}
\rangle
+
\frac{L_i}{2}
\|\mathbf{B}-\mathbf{C}\|_F^2.
$$

Apply this inequality twice: once with the previous factor $\mathbf{A}\_i^k$ as the comparison point, and once with an optimal factor $\mathbf{A}\_i^\star$ as the comparison point. Weight the first inequality by $t_{k+1}-1$, add the second, and use

$$
t_k^2
=
t_{k+1}^2-t_{k+1}.
$$

The extrapolation step is chosen so that the inner-product terms combine into a difference of squared distances. Summing from iteration $0$ through $k-1$ makes those distances telescope. For a fixed block subproblem, this yields the familiar accelerated estimate

$$
F_i\!\left(\mathbf{A}_i^k\right)
-
F_i\!\left(\mathbf{A}_i^\star\right)
\leq
\frac{
2L_i
\left\|\mathbf{A}_i^0-\mathbf{A}_i^\star\right\|_F^2
}{
(k+1)^2
}.
$$

Because

$$
t_k
\geq
\frac{k+1}{2},
$$

the objective gap decays as $O(1/k^2)$.

The paper extends this blockwise argument across the alternating sweep and states the following result.

**Theorem.** Let

$$
\mathcal{A}^k
:=
\left(
\mathbf{A}_1^k,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N^k
\right)
$$

be generated by our algorithm, and let

$$
\mathcal{A}^\star
:=
\left(
\mathbf{A}_1^\star,\ldots,\mathbf{A}_N^\star
\right)
$$

be an optimal factorization. Under the stated smoothness, strong-convexity, step-size, and benign-landscape assumptions,

$$
F\!\left(\mathcal{A}^k\right)
-
F\!\left(\mathcal{A}^\star\right)
=
\mathcal{O}\!\left(\frac{1}{k^2}\right).
$$

The assumptions matter. The objective is block convex but jointly nonconvex, so the per-block accelerated rate alone does not automatically establish a global rate for an arbitrary CP problem. The theorem relies on the paper's landscape assumption that the relevant low-rank completion problem has no spurious local minima, together with valid block step sizes throughout the sweep. Within that setting, acceleration improves the $O(1/k)$ first-order rate to $O(1/k^2)$ in objective gap.

## What the Experiments Show

The experiments evaluate the method on matrix completion, color images, grayscale video, and color video. Recovery quality is measured by relative squared error

$$
\operatorname{RSE}
=
\frac{
\left\|
\mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{recovered}}
-
\mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{original}}
\right\|_F
}{
\left\|
\mathcal{X}_{\mathrm{original}}
\right\|_F
}.
$$

Lower is better.

### Rank Ablation

The target CP rank $R$ controls both expressiveness and cost. A rank that is too small cannot represent the data well. Increasing $R$ improves the reconstruction and early convergence, but also makes each iteration more expensive.

### Video Recovery

The grayscale video experiment uses 92 frames of size $512\times384$ and removes $90\%$ of all entries uniformly at random. The recovered tensor reconstructs the static piano and background well. Motion is more difficult: the pianist's head and hands are blurred because a small CP rank favors features that remain consistent across frames.

The paper also treats a color video as a fourth-order tensor with height, width, color, and time modes.

This example makes the low-rank assumption intuitive. A stationary pixel pattern appears repeatedly over time, so it is unlikely to be missing in every frame. Moving objects do not align in the same coordinates and are therefore harder to represent with a small number of shared components.

Across a separate comparison with BATF, BGCP, BPMF, BTMF, BTRMF, and TRMF, ATC reports an error of $0.135$ in $0.840$ seconds. The next lowest reported error is $1.463$, and the next fastest runtime is $6.368$ seconds. The absolute values depend on the experimental setup, but the joint error-time advantage supports the paper's main computational claim.

## Takeaways

The main modeling lesson is that tensor completion does not require a single monolithic tensor SVD. A CP decomposition turns every mode unfolding into a matrix factorization, and the Burer--Monteiro identity turns each matrix nuclear norm into a penalty on the shared factors. Combining all modes gives one regularizer that respects the multidimensional structure while avoiding repeated spectral decompositions.

The main optimization lesson is that a nonconvex problem can still have simple convex blocks. Once all factors except $\mathbf{A}_i$ are fixed, the subproblem has a Lipschitz gradient and strong convexity supplied by the factor regularizer. That structure makes an accelerated gradient update natural and cheap.

The target rank remains the central practical trade-off. A rank that is too small underfits; a very large rank raises the cost of every sweep. The experiments suggest that the method is fairly stable after the rank crosses a modest threshold, which makes a small rank grid a reasonable way to tune it.

Finally, the upper-bound interpretation is important. For matrices, factorized Frobenius regularization is exactly equivalent to the nuclear norm when the factor width is sufficient. For higher-order tensors, the shared CP factors generally cannot minimize every unfolding penalty simultaneously. The proposed regularizer is valuable because it is a tractable, mode-aware surrogate, not because it removes the inherent difficulty of tensor rank.
